Pre-frontal Cortex

What is it?

The pre-frontal cortex covers the anterior portion of the frontal lobe and is a part of the cerebral cortex or is the area right behind the forehead. This region is considered integral to personality, decision-making, problem solving, planning, processing complex thoughts or behavior, and moderating social behavior.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

If this area becomes damaged a person’s judgments or decision-making could become impaired. Carrying out complex tasks and the ability to effectively plan or organize may be effected as well. An individual with this injury may not be able to recognize socially acceptable behavior, act or say inappropriate things, lack empathy for others, and show reduced impulse control. These can as a result impact one’s relationships and can alter the personality of the individual.

Parietal Lobe

What is it?

This structure is located directly behind the frontal lobe or toward the back of the head directly under the skull bone. It is responsible for processing all sensory information from various parts of the body such as taste, touch, and temperature. It plays an essential role in controlling sensation and helping us orient ourselves in the world around us through spatial processing. Language and memory are also important aspects that the parietal lobe controls.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injury can thus result in difficulty recognizing and locating one’s body parts or body as a whole. This can cause impairment with many self-care skills, such as dressing and washing. Difficulty in being able to make things, draw, recognize people or objects, and denial of one’s present deficits may also be seen in an affected individual.

Cerebellum

What is it?

This area is found near the base of the brain. The cerebellum is essential to learning and performing skilled, coordinated movements. This structure is involved in balance, muscle tone, equilibrium, and voluntary movement. Essentially, it is what helps us to be able to ride a bike and plays a role in attention as well.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this area can thus result in reduced coordination in movement, loss of ability to walk, difficulties in the speed of movement, and inability to grab objects.

Occipital Lobe

What is it?

The occipital lobe can be found at the very back of the brain, behind the parietal lobe. Its primary function is responsible for vision. More specifically it has areas that aid in interpreting color and language by our visual perceptions.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this area can result in visual impairments and other vision related issues. This can range from visual hallucinations and illusions to total loss of vision or blindness. In addition, this can lead to difficulties interpreting language and reading comprehension. Blurry vision, dizziness, headaches, sudden onset of anger, and changes in sleeping patterns may also be seen.

Temporal Lobe

What is it?

The temporal lobes can be found along the sides of the brain under the parietal lobes or lie about ear level behind the frontal lobes. They are responsible for hearing and all auditory functions. This includes recognizing and processing sound, understanding as well as producing speech, and has a role in memory processing.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injury to this area may affect one’s hearing, language, facial recognition abilities, and the capability to process sensory information. This may be seen in the individual expressing impairment to comprehend language, inability to pay attention to what they have heard or even seen, have experiences of auditory sensation as well perceptual disturbances, and suffer from memory loss.

Hippocampus

What is it?

The hippocampus plays important role in the processing of memory. It helps consolidate short-term memory to long-term memory and is important in spatial memory for navigation. This structure helps us orient ourselves in our environment, aids in the creation of new memories, facilitates navigation in our surroundings, and helps retain memories we have created.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this region may cause confusion, disorientation, mood changes, memory loss, or even inability to form new memories.

Amygdala

What is it?

We find this structure near the hippocampus in the front portion of the temporal lobes. It is involved in recognition of dangerous or fearful situations, the formation and storage of emotional events, the formation of long-term memory, and helps us learn from pleasure responses.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Signs of damage to this area can be seen in problems with learning and retention, lack of emotional sensitivity, challenges with memory formation, a reduction in fear and aggression, expressing greater risk taking behavior, and the development of depression as well as anxiety.

Basal Ganglia

What is it?

This structure is involved with initiating voluntary movements, particularly sequences of movement. It is also associated with procedural learning (such as routine behaviors or habits), eye movements, and plays a role in motivation.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

A person with basal ganglia dysfunction may present problems with movement that range from difficulty sustaining movement to uncontrollable or repeated movements. Muscle spasms and rigidity may result, making behaviors such as walking or typing a challenge. Memory loss and trouble finding words while speaking may be seen. Inability to control one’s speech and posture, along with exhibiting tremors may also result.

Brainstem

What is it?

The brainstem is very essential as it controls life-sustaining functions. Located at the base of the brain this regulates involuntary functions such as breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and swallowing. In addition, this helps us with alertness and sensation.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injuries to the brainstem may affect the previously listed functions, cause problems with motor coordination, issues with balance, facial paralysis, interruptions in sensations, and in severe cases elicit death or coma.

Medulla

What is it?

This structure is a part of the brainstem and assists in life sustaining functions. It helps control the functions of breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and the reflexes for swallowing and vomiting.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injury to the medulla is difficult to treat as it is involved with controlling vital functions. Impacts of damage to this structure are severe and usually result in death.

Pons

What is it?

The pons is a portion of the brain stem that relays signals or information to the cerebellum. The information it transfers is involved in the regulation of sleep, respiration, swallowing, bladder control, hearing, equilibrium, taste, eye movement, facial expression, and posture.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injury to this area may cause difficulties with balance, dizziness, problems swallowing, issues with articulating words, numbness, weakness in half of the body, and further complications depending on the severity of damage.

Broca's Area

What is it?

This is a region located in the frontal lobe of the dominant hemisphere of the brain, which in most cases is the left hemisphere. It is essential for speech production or language articulation.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this region would display problems with speaking and motor speech control. Individuals with injury to this area can still understand language, however have difficulty forming the words to speak fluently. Speech may be slow, lack correct grammar, mostly contain simplistic words, and the individual may have difficulty repeating words or phrases.

Wernicke's Area

What is it?

Located in the left temporal lobe this area is responsible for the comprehension of speech, language recognition, semantic processing, and interpretation.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this region results in difficulty processing and understanding spoken or written language. Individuals with injury to this area are able to speak words and form sentences with grammatical correctness, however these sentences do not make sense. In other words these individuals may use completely unrelated or irrelevant words that have no meaning in the sentence or conversation. Often times the affected individual is unaware that their speech lacks any sense.

Hypothalamus

What is it?

Located below the thalamus and above the brain stem is the hypothalamus. This structure is essential to a variety of functions from regulation of body temperature, aiding in the control of hunger and thirst, regulation of mood, aids growth, controls sleep, and the release of essential hormones that regulate normal body function.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Injury to this region can effect emotions, thirst, hunger, sleep, and sex drive. Hormone and appetite disturbances, along with chronic fatigue can result from this damage. Severe damage can be considered fatal, as this can offset homeostasis or the stable state of conditions in which the body must remain in order to properly function.

Thalamus

What is it?

This is the main relay center through which incoming sensory information passes before it is sent along to the parietal lobe for interpretation of the sensation. The thalamus is also important in the regulation of awake or sleep states, pain perception, motor control, and memory formation.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to the thalamus can cause insomnia, loss of sensation or feeling, paralysis, involuntary muscle contractions, and in severe cases a permanent coma.


Rehabilitation in Action

Pituitary Gland

What is it?

This structure sits just below the hypothalamus and is the primary hormone releasing structure in the brain. It assists the hypothalamus in controlling hunger, thirst, sleep, and body temperature.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Head injury that results in damage to this structure can reduce the production of two important hormones for normal function. Decreased growth hormone production can lead to fatigue, reduced stamina, anxiety, and depression. Reduction in producing antidiuretic hormone, as a result of pituitary damage, can cause diabetes insipidus (generates excessive urination and extreme thirst).

Pre-central Gyrus

What is it?

Considered the “motor strip” located on the posterior surface of the frontal lobe. This acts as the main motor center in the initiation of voluntary movement by contraction of skeletal muscle.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this area can result in the loss of motor control over the body.

Post-central Gyrus

What is it?

This structure lies on the anterior edge of the parietal lobe and is called the “sensory strip.” This is the main sensory receptive area for the sensation of touch.


What happens when it becomes damaged?

Damage to this area can lead to loss of sensation in terms of the ability to feel fine touch and vibration. A reduction in feeling pain or heat can also result.
